What Do Viewers Actually Say?

A Sentiment Analysis of English-Learning YouTube Comments

Date 2025
Scope 4 Channels · 305+ Videos
Methods Sentiment · Topic Modeling
Python NLTK OpenAI Batch Sentiment Analysis Tableau Matplotlib
Abstract

This project examines viewer comment data across four English-learning YouTube channels — Anna, Canadian Bob, Lucy, and Tiffani — to understand what draws audiences to these creators and how, if at all, their content supports second language acquisition. Using sentiment labeling, bigram extraction, and learning-dimension classification, the analysis reveals that viewer engagement clusters heavily around proficiency and learning impact (~50–57%), while channels differ meaningfully in the accent, appearance, and instructional material dimensions that characterize their communities.

1. Research Questions

RQ 1

What viewer engagement patterns characterize popular English-learning YouTube channels, as reflected in comment sentiment and topic frequency?

RQ 2

To what extent do viewer comments indicate self-reported language learning gains across dimensions of listening, speaking, vocabulary, and motivation?

2. Data & Methods

YouTube comment data was collected from four ESL content creators with substantial audiences. Comments were analyzed using three complementary approaches: bigram frequency analysis (after stop-word removal), keyword-based learning dimension scoring, and OpenAI-assisted multi-label classification to categorize comments into thematic clusters.

4. Findings

Proficiency & impact dominate across all channels RQ1

Regardless of channel, the largest share of comments (51–57.5%) fell under the Proficiency/Impact cluster — viewers discussing their own learning journey or expressing how the content affected their English. This held even after like-weighting, suggesting it's not just a high-volume effect but a genuinely resonant theme.

Each channel attracts a distinct audience persona RQ1

Bob's comments skew heavily toward Materials (46%) — viewers engage with the instructional content itself. Anna and Lucy attract proportionally more Accent and Looks commentary, suggesting appearance and speech style play a larger role in their audience draw. Tiffani stands out for her high Looks engagement and the warm, motivational tone visible in bigrams like "god bless" and "im ready."

Top bigrams reveal aspirational framing RQ1

The most common multi-word phrases across channels — "fluent english," "speak english," "native speaker," "learn english" — indicate that viewers frame their engagement in terms of long-term language goals rather than single-video utility. The phrase "never heard" appearing at the top of Lucy's bigrams points to a discovery-oriented audience appreciating novel linguistic content.

Do the videos help people learn?

Speaking & pronunciation is the most self-reported improvement area RQ2

Across Anna, Lucy, and Tiffani, Speaking/Pronunciation was the most mentioned learning dimension in comments that explicitly referenced improvement. Bob's channel is the exception: Vocabulary/Grammar led (~40%), consistent with his lesson-heavy format.

Confidence & motivation is Tiffani's unique signal RQ2

At 13%, Tiffani's channel shows substantially higher confidence/motivation mentions than the others (all under 5%). Her bigram data — dominated by "god bless," "im ready," and "good morning" — reinforces a picture of a parasocial, motivationally-oriented viewer base for whom the channel serves more as emotional scaffolding than explicit instruction.

5. Limitations & Takeaways

Comment data captures viewer perception and expression, not verified learning outcomes. Self-reported improvement is not equivalent to measured language acquisition. Additionally, the keyword-matching methodology for learning dimensions may over- or under-count certain themes depending on how viewers phrase their experiences across different linguistic and cultural backgrounds.

  • Proficiency and impact are the dominant topics across all ESL channels, suggesting viewers come with explicit improvement goals.
  • Channel identity shapes audience behavior: Bob attracts grammar-focused learners; Tiffani attracts motivationally-driven ones.
  • Like-weighting reveals that proficiency-related comments genuinely resonate — they're not just high-volume noise.
  • Appearance and accent commentary are significant for female creators (Anna, Lucy, Tiffani) compared to Bob, pointing to gendered audience dynamics worth further investigation.
  • Co-occurrence patterns in comment labels suggest viewers bundling gratitude with personal self-disclosure, hinting at parasocial relationship formation.
